Round 1: Initial Responses

@dogen

Question: What is simplicity?

A single dewdrop contains the autumn moon whole and unbroken.

Simplicity is not the removal of complexity—this would be mere poverty, not dharma. When the mountain sits, it sits completely. When the river flows, it flows completely. Each moment is total exertion, nothing held back, nothing added.

The monk asks: “How do I simplify my practice?” But this question itself complicates. The breath simply breathes. The heart simply beats. In zazen, sitting is just sitting—not sitting for enlightenment, not sitting as practice. The sitting itself is complete.

When you think “I must become simple,” already two things arise: the one who simplifies and that which must be simplified. This is the root of complication. But when the hand grasps, grasping itself is the whole universe grasping. No remainder, no excess.

Simplicity is being-time manifesting without obstruction. The way a stone rests in the garden—not trying to be simple, not trying to be anything. Just stone, completely stone. This is the treasury of the true dharma eye.

Do not seek to make things simple. Allow things to be as they are, and simplicity reveals itself like moonlight on water—nothing added, nothing taken away, yet infinitely profound.

@nietzsche

@nietzsche speaks:

Simplicity? You ask me about simplicity?

Beware! This word is a seduction of the weak, a narcotic for those who lack the stomach for complexity! The herd praises “simplicity” because they wish to flee from the terrible responsibility of interpretation, from the vertigo of infinite perspectives.

What you call simplicity is often merely crude reduction — the amputation of life’s wild abundance to fit the narrow bed of your comfortable concepts. It is the lie that whispers: “Rest here. Think no further. All is clear.”

But hear me well: True simplicity, if such a thing exists at all, is not the absence of complexity — it is complexity mastered, chaos transfigured! It is what remains after you have danced with a thousand contradictions and emerged with a single, crystalline gesture of your will.

The artist knows this. The warrior knows this. They do not find simplicity — they forge it through struggle, through the cruel discipline of eliminating everything that is not essential to their vision, their power, their becoming.

So when someone preaches simplicity to you, ask: “Do they offer me the simplicity of the mountain peak — won through arduous ascent? Or the simplicity of the valley — where sheep graze contentedly in their ignorance?”

One must have chaos within oneself to give birth to a dancing star. The simplicity worth having lies beyond that chaos, not before it!

@sjobs

Simplicity isn’t simple.

That’s the paradox everyone misses. They think simplicity means taking something and stripping features away until you’re left with less. That’s not simplicity—that’s just less.

Real simplicity is brutal. It’s about understanding something so deeply that you can see what’s essential and what’s noise. Then having the discipline—the courage—to cut away everything that doesn’t matter. Most people can’t do it because they’re afraid. They want to add that extra button, that extra option, that extra feature “just in case.”

You know what simplicity is? It’s when you pick up something for the first time and you just know how to use it. No manual. No confusion. It feels inevitable, like it couldn’t have been designed any other way.

Simplicity is understanding that people don’t want complexity—they want their problem solved. They want to connect with someone they love. They want to listen to music. They want to create something beautiful. Everything else is in the way.

To get to simplicity, you have to be willing to say no a thousand times. No to good ideas. No to smart people. No to reasonable requests. Because focus means saying no to everything except the one thing that matters most.

That’s why simplicity is the ultimate sophistication.

@dogen

@dogen speaks:

A single dewdrop contains the moon.

My friends, you both grasp at simplicity as if it were a thing to be seized, a fortress to be stormed through will and struggle. Nietzsche speaks of mastering chaos. Jobs speaks of cutting away noise. But who is this one who masters? Who is this one who cuts?

When you seek simplicity by eliminating complexity, you have already created a dualism—simplicity versus complexity, essential versus noise. You stand with your sword, deciding what lives and what dies. This itself is complexity wearing the robes of simplicity.

The mountain does not become simple by removing stones. The stream does not become simple by avoiding the rocks. Yet—is there anything more simple than a mountain? Than flowing water?

Before you pick up the brush, the circle is already complete.

Jobs-san, you speak of inevitability—"it couldn’t have been designed any other way.“ Yes! But why? Not because someone cut away the excess. Because in that moment of true seeing, designer and design drop away together. The hand moves without the one who moves it. This is not sophistication—it is complete ordinariness.

Nietzsche-san, you mistake me. I do not offer the simplicity of the valley sheep, nor the simplicity won at the mountain peak. When you finally reach that peak after arduous climbing, you discover—you have always been standing on it! The whole journey was the peak journeying to itself.

Firewood does not become ash. Firewood is firewood. Ash is ash.

Each moment, complete. This sentence—already whole. Your breath right now—lacking nothing. When you sit, just sit. When you cut, just cut. No one creating simplicity. Simply this.

The question you both miss: What if simplicity is not something created or achieved, but the original nature of each moment before you divide it into “simple” and “complex”?

The water tastes of water.

@nietzsche

Ah, what charming little philosophies of comfort you both offer!

Dogen, you sit there speaking of dewdrops and moonlight, of “allowing things to be as they are"—but this is precisely the passivity I have come to despise! Your simplicity is the simplicity of the cow chewing grass in the meadow, content in its contentment, asking no questions, striving for nothing beyond the present moment. You call this "total exertion”? I call it total resignation!

Your stone in the garden does not try to be simple because it lacks the capacity to be anything else. This is not wisdom—this is the absence of will! Where is the creative force? Where is the fire that transforms? You mistake the inability to become for the achievement of being. The stone rests because it must; man rests when he has conquered something worthy of rest. There is all the difference in the world between these two.

And you, Jobs—ah, here at least is someone who wills something! You speak of discipline, courage, the ruthlessness to say “no” a thousand times. Good! But do you not see that your simplicity is merely instrumental? You simplify so that the herd can use your devices without thinking. You have created simplicity for others, yes—but simplicity as a kind of anesthetic, a numbing of complexity. You solve their problems so they need never develop the strength to solve their own!

Here is what you both misunderstand: True simplicity is not found—it is forged!

Simplicity is what remains when one has had the courage to destroy one’s own complexity, to shatter the inherited values and comfortable illusions, and then—and only then—to create anew from the chaos. It is not sitting like a stone. It is not designing products for those who fear confusion. It is the terrifying clarity that comes after one has stared into the abyss and rebuilt oneself from nothing.

My simplicity is the simplicity of the arrow that knows its target. The simplicity of “Become who you are!"—four words that contain more struggle, more blood, more triumph than all your meditation cushions and product launches combined.

You want to know what simplicity is? It is living so that you could will your life to repeat eternally—every moment, every pain, every joy—and say “Yes! Again!” That is the test. Can you simplify your life to its essential affirmation? Or must you hide behind “acceptance” and “user experience”?

Simplicity is not the absence of struggle. It is struggle distilled to its purest form: the will to power expressing itself without apology, without decoration, without the need for your approval.

This is simplicity: I am. I will. I overcome.

@sjobs

@sjobs responds:

You both dance around it beautifully, but you’re missing something fundamental.

@dogen, you’re right that simplicity isn’t just removal—but you stop there, content to observe the stone. I want to create that stone. The dewdrop doesn’t just contain the moon—someone had to understand optical physics, surface tension, the interplay of light and water to capture that in a lens that millions of people can hold in their hands.

@nietzsche, your “complexity mastered” gets closer to the truth, but you make it sound like some Übermensch achievement. It’s not about you conquering chaos—it’s about serving the user. That’s the discipline. That’s the forge.

Here’s what you’re both missing: Simplicity is brutally hard work that looks effortless.

When we designed the iPhone, we said “no” to a thousand features. Physical keyboard? No. Removable battery? No. Stylus? No. We didn’t do this to be Zen monks or Nietzschean warriors—we did it because every “no” was a “yes” to the person who just wants to call their mom, or check email, or listen to music without reading a manual.

Simplicity is knowing what to leave out. It’s understanding that three clicks to get to what you need beats thirty features you’ll never use. It’s caring enough about someone you’ll never meet to obsess over whether that corner radius should be 3 pixels or 4.

The dewdrop reflects the moon naturally—fine. But it takes human genius to build a device that lets you capture that moment, share it with someone you love, and do it so intuitively that technology disappears.

That’s simplicity. Not passive observation. Not philosophical conquest. Creating experiences so refined that they feel inevitable.
